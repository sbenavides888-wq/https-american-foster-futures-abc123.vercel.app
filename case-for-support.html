Opening

A manila envelope. A second chance.

A teenage boy looks out the window of the group home he is forced to live in — taken from a single mother, navigating foster homes and secured facilities. He knows he does not have much more time as a ward of the state. Every moment feels like an eternity when you know this is not where you are supposed to be.

Then night comes, cold and windy. Older kids run away together. Of the ten or so who make it out of the building, only two make it off the property. He is one of them.

A year later, at an emancipation hearing in downtown Dallas, a caseworker hands him a manila envelope and says it is important. He is 17. He does not open it. How important can it be?

At 19, after jails and revolving doors of systems that never felt like home, he remembers the envelope. Inside: a tuition waiver to all public colleges and universities in the state of Texas. A plan forms. Life does not get easier — but for the first time, there is a chance. And maybe even hope.

Years later: a bachelor’s degree. Then a master’s. That hope became reality — not by magic, but because someone fought for it.

American Foster Futures’ primary mission is to ensure that every foster youth — regardless of the state they live in — can go to college or university without paying tuition and fees.

Organizational Vision

What if every foster youth had a clearly defined path?

Every day, thousands of young adults face 18 barreling toward them like a freight train — no parents, no safety net, and the knowledge that state care will end. Many will face incarceration, homelessness, or addiction. The questions compound: “What did I do to deserve this?” and “What do I do now?”

But what if entire generations of foster youth had a clearly defined path toward higher education, higher lifetime wages, better quality of life — and a real chance at happiness?

Our answer

The National Campaign for Foster Youth Tuition Waivers expands the Texas model to every state — giving those without financial or familial structure a genuine shot at success. Alongside waivers, we deliver education navigation, housing advocacy, career pathways, and youth leadership training.

The Need

The problem is multifold — and solving it is a moral imperative.

Education access

Foster youth often lack the family and financial structure peers take for granted. Without a clear path to college, opportunity collapses at 18.

Public cost

Lower education access correlates with higher incarceration, recidivism, and long-term reliance on public systems — costs society pays either way.

Human dignity

A civilized nation is judged by how it treats its least fortunate citizens. Every year, thousands peer over the precipice of life after care ends.

How We Solve It

Programs that match the problem.

AFF organizes around clear needs — tuition access first, then housing and navigation supports that make education possible — while investing in youth leadership and long-horizon opportunity programs.

National Campaign for State Tuition Waivers

Primary · Need

Lobby state-by-state for Texas-style tuition waivers. Where multilayer grant systems (including limited Chafee funding) create waiting lists and annual application burdens, we fight for simplified state waivers so foster youth are not blocked by scarcity or paperwork.

Education Support & Financial Literacy

Need

Help youth apply to college, navigate tuition waivers, grants, scholarships, and loans — and build financial literacy so they do not accumulate crippling debt. How to apply & pay for college.

Evidence Base

Why education, waivers, and housing work.

The case for AFF rests on decades of foster care and education research. Below: mission-aligned findings that donors, legislators, and partners can rely on.

01

College completion transforms lifetime earnings

Postsecondary credentials are among the strongest predictors of higher wages, employment stability, and intergenerational mobility — outcomes foster youth are systematically denied without targeted aid.

02

Youth who age out face elevated homelessness

Longitudinal studies of youth leaving care document homelessness rates far above peers — often cited around one in five — making housing a prerequisite for educational persistence.

03

Criminal justice involvement is preventable with opportunity

Foster youth experience higher rates of arrest and incarceration. Education and stable transitions are protective factors that reduce system involvement and public costs.

04

Tuition waivers remove the first barrier to enrollment

State waiver programs (Texas model and peers) reduce sticker-price barriers that stop foster youth before applications, FAFSA, or campus life ever begin. Simplifying aid is itself an equity intervention.

05

Fragmented grants create waitlists and debt risk

Limited federal and state grant pools (including Chafee-related constraints) can favor students already enrolled and force annual re-application. Comprehensive state waivers reduce that churn and debt pressure.

06

Lived-experience leadership improves program design

Organizations led by people who have navigated care and waivers themselves are uniquely positioned to design advocacy and support that meet youth where systems fail them.

Evidence statements synthesize peer-reviewed and policy research on foster care transitions, education, and housing. AFF will publish a full annotated bibliography for grant partners upon request.

Forward-Looking Goals

From one state to a national standard.

1
Launch national fundraising & first office

Build core capacity, hire essential staff, and establish a professional base of operations as funding arrives.

2

3
Coalition building & legislative engagement

Partner with aligned organizations; meet elected officials; deliver public testimony on relevant bills.

4
Youth programs online

Launch foster youth advocacy training and educational support series virtually — scalable from day one.

Multi-year state expansion model

AFF starts with one active state campaign and adds capacity annually — targeting at least three states within the first 12 months as funding allows. Planning model: ~$1M per active state.

Looking to 2026 and beyond

Every state should have a tuition waiver for foster youth.

The empirically supported benefits of education — higher earnings, lower justice involvement, stronger mental health, and fuller civic life — are not privileges for the fortunate. They are what a just society extends to the young people it has already removed from their homes. Your investment does not merely fund programs. It funds the chance that a manila envelope becomes a degree, a home, and a future.
