Financial Aid Guide

How to Apply and Pay for College

A practical roadmap for current and former foster youth — from applications to stacking waivers, grants, and scholarships.

Only about 20% of foster youth enroll in college. Cost is a major barrier — but multiple aid sources can be combined. Start early and keep documentation of your foster care history.

1

Apply to colleges or trade programs

Research schools with foster youth support offices. Apply to public institutions in states with strong waiver programs when possible. Confirm admission deadlines and housing options.

2

Complete the FAFSA

Former foster youth are often considered independent students — no parent income required. This unlocks Pell Grants and campus aid. File at studentaid.gov as soon as it opens each year.

3

Secure your state tuition waiver

Many states offer partial or full tuition waivers for foster youth. Texas has one of the strongest programs — see our Texas waiver guide. Request eligibility verification from your state child welfare agency and submit forms to the financial aid office.

4

Apply for Chafee ETV and campus grants

The federal Chafee Education and Training Voucher provides up to $5,000/year for tuition, books, housing, and transportation. Contact your state's ETV coordinator early — deadlines vary.

5

Stack aid and plan for living costs

Waivers, Pell Grants, and ETV can often be combined. Budget for housing, food, and transportation — not just tuition. Ask your campus foster youth liaison about emergency funds and year-round support.

Need help navigating the process?

AFF helps foster youth understand waivers, complete applications, and connect with campus resources nationwide.
